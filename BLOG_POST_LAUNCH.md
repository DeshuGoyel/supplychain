# Why Supply Chain Software Is Fragmented (And How We Fixed It)

*Published: January 5, 2024*

If you're running a supply chain operation, you probably have a problem you don't even realize you have. Your data is scattered across 5-10 different software tools, your team spends 40% of their time on manual data entry, and your executives make decisions based on insights that are 2-3 days old.

Sound familiar? You're not alone.

## The Problem: Data Silos Are Killing Your Supply Chain

### The Typical Enterprise Stack

Most mid-market companies ($100M-$2B revenue) use a fragmented supply chain software stack that looks something like this:

- **SAP or Oracle** for inventory management ($15K-$50K/year)
- **Kinaxis or Blue Yonder** for demand planning ($25K-$75K/year)
- **FourKites or project44** for visibility and tracking ($20K-$60K/year)
- **Tableau or Power BI** for analytics and reporting ($10K-$30K/year)
- **Email, Excel, and Slack** for everything else (priceless in frustration)

**Total cost: $70K-$215K per year** just in software licenses, not including implementation, training, and maintenance costs.

### The Hidden Costs of Fragmentation

But the real cost isn't just the license fees. Here's what's really happening:

#### 1. **Manual Data Entry Eats 40% of Your Team's Time**
Your supply chain team spends nearly half their day manually copying data from one system to another. They export inventory data from SAP, import it into Excel, analyze it, then manually update demand forecasts in your planning tool. This isn't just inefficient—it's error-prone.

#### 2. **Decision Lag of 2-3 Days**
When something goes wrong (a supplier delay, inventory shortage, demand spike), it takes 2-3 days for that information to flow through all your systems and reach decision makers. In today's supply chain environment, that's an eternity.

#### 3. **No Single Source of Truth**
Your CFO sees inventory data from SAP, your VP Operations sees different numbers from your planning system, and your warehouse manager has yet another version in their Excel spreadsheet. Who's right? Nobody knows for sure.

#### 4. **Missed Opportunities Cost More Than Software**
- **Stockouts**: Happen because visibility is delayed
- **Overstock**: Capital tied up in inventory that could be used elsewhere  
- **Supplier Delays**: Discovered too late to mitigate
- **Lost Sales**: From inventory positions you didn't know you had

A typical mid-size manufacturer loses $500K-$2M annually from these avoidable supply chain issues.

## The Cost: Why Companies Tolerate This Mess

### "It's Always Been This Way"
Supply chain leaders have been working with fragmented systems for so long that they assume this complexity is normal. When asked about their tech stack, they often say "we use [tool A] for this, [tool B] for that, and Excel for everything else" like it's a badge of honor rather than a system failure.

### "Integration Is Too Hard"
Traditional enterprise software companies have trained buyers to expect complex, multi-year implementations with custom integrations. The result? Companies buy best-of-breed point solutions and live with the fragmentation because they believe integration is too expensive or complex.

### "Our Data Is Too Complex"
Every supply chain feels unique, and vendors have convinced companies that they need highly customized solutions. The reality? Most supply chains follow similar patterns, and companies need standardization more than customization.
